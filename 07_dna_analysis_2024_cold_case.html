CASE BACKGROUND

On November 14, 1957, Miss Vivienne Marchand, a 32-year-old jazz singer, was found deceased in her private sleeper compartment aboard the Silver Comet passenger train traveling from Chicago to New Orleans. Autopsy determined cause of death to be homicide by ligature strangulation. Despite immediate investigation, the case went cold due to lack of physical evidence and limited forensic capabilities of the era. The murder weapon—a silk scarf worn by the victim—was never recovered in 1957.

In November 2024, the New Orleans Police Department was contacted by the estate executor of Charlotte Devereaux Beaumont (deceased October 2023, age 95). During estate clearing, a locked steamer trunk was discovered in the attic of the deceased's residence at 3847 Prytania Street, New Orleans. Inside the trunk, investigators found a burgundy and gold silk scarf wrapped in tissue paper, along with personal journals, photographs, and correspondence. Based on witness descriptions from the 1957 investigation, this scarf was identified as potentially belonging to the victim, Vivienne Marchand, and possibly constituting the murder weapon.

The scarf was submitted to the Louisiana State Police Crime Laboratory for DNA analysis on November 18, 2024. This report details the findings of that analysis.

EVIDENCE DESCRIPTION

Item #1: One (1) silk scarf, measuring approximately 36 inches by 36 inches. Burgundy red base color with gold paisley pattern. Designer label: "Hermès Paris." Fabric composition: 100% silk. The scarf exhibits signs of age-appropriate deterioration but remains intact. Visual inspection reveals multiple areas of potential biological staining (brownish discoloration) consistent with aged blood or bodily fluids.

The scarf was stored in acid-free tissue paper within a sealed wooden trunk for approximately 67 years (1957-2024). Storage conditions were suboptimal (attic environment with temperature fluctuations), but the sealed trunk provided protection from UV light and external contamination. This preservation allowed for potential DNA recovery.

ANALYTICAL METHODS

Sample Collection

Seventeen (17) discrete areas of visible staining were identified on the scarf and systematically sampled using sterile cotton swabs moistened with sterile distilled water. Each sample area was photographed and documented prior to collection. Samples were assigned laboratory reference numbers LSP-DNA-2024-11-1847-A through LSP-DNA-2024-11-1847-Q.

DNA Extraction

DNA extraction was performed using the QIAamp DNA Investigator Kit (Qiagen), optimized for degraded and aged biological samples. Extraction followed manufacturer protocols with extended incubation times to maximize DNA recovery from aged biological material.

DNA Quantification

Quantification of extracted DNA was performed using the Quantifiler Trio DNA Quantification Kit (Applied Biosystems) on a 7500 Real-Time PCR instrument. Quantification results indicated the presence of human DNA in 14 of the 17 sampled areas, with concentrations ranging from 0.008 ng/µL to 0.142 ng/µL.

DNA Amplification and Profiling

DNA profiling was conducted using the PowerPlex Fusion 6C System (Promega), which amplifies 27 autosomal STR loci, 3 Y-STR loci, and Amelogenin for sex determination. Amplification was performed using 29 PCR cycles optimized for low-template and degraded DNA. Capillary electrophoresis was performed on a 3500xL Genetic Analyzer (Applied Biosystems). Data analysis was conducted using GeneMapper ID-X software v1.6.

This profile was compared to reference DNA obtained from preserved autopsy tissue samples from the 1957 investigation (archived at Jackson City Hospital, Mississippi). INTERPRETATION

The DNA analysis results provide definitive forensic evidence linking Charlotte Devereaux to the 1957 murder of Vivienne Marchand. The presence of two distinct female DNA profiles on the silk scarf—one belonging to the victim and one belonging to Charlotte Devereaux—supports the following conclusions:

  1. Murder Weapon Identification: The burgundy and gold Hermès silk scarf is confirmed as the ligature used to strangle Vivienne Marchand on November 14, 1957. The victim's DNA is present on the scarf in areas consistent with neck contact during strangulation.
  2. Suspect Identification: Charlotte Devereaux's DNA is present on the scarf in areas consistent with hand grip positions at both ends of the scarf. This indicates she physically handled the scarf with sufficient contact to transfer epithelial cells (touch DNA).
  3. Temporal Correlation: The scarf was missing from the crime scene in 1957 and was recovered from Charlotte Devereaux's residence in 2024. This chain of custody, combined with DNA evidence, indicates Charlotte Devereaux removed the scarf from the crime scene after the murder and retained possession of it until her death.
  4. Exclusion of Alternative Explanations: The concentration and distribution of Charlotte Devereaux's DNA on the scarf (particularly on grip areas) is inconsistent with casual or incidental contact. The DNA evidence supports direct involvement in the use of the scarf as a murder weapon.

STATISTICAL ANALYSIS

The probability of the DNA profile attributed to Charlotte Devereaux originating from an unrelated, random individual in the general population is calculated as follows:

  • Caucasian Population Database: 1 in 476 trillion (4.76 × 10¹⁴)
  • African American Population Database: 1 in 892 trillion (8.92 × 10¹⁴)
  • Hispanic Population Database: 1 in 523 trillion (5.23 × 10¹⁴)

These statistics far exceed the threshold for forensic certainty (typically 1 in 1 billion). The DNA match to Charlotte Devereaux is conclusive.

CONCLUSIONS

  1. The burgundy and gold silk scarf recovered from the residence of Charlotte Devereaux Beaumont in November 2024 is identified as the murder weapon used in the 1957 homicide of Vivienne Marchand.
  2. DNA analysis confirms the presence of both the victim's DNA (Vivienne Marchand) and the suspect's DNA (Charlotte Devereaux Beaumont) on the scarf.
  3. The DNA evidence, combined with the possession of the murder weapon for 67 years, provides conclusive forensic proof that Charlotte Devereaux committed the murder of Vivienne Marchand on November 14, 1957.
  4. This cold case is considered forensically solved based on DNA evidence.

LIMITATIONS AND CONSIDERATIONS

While DNA evidence provides conclusive identification, the following limitations are noted:

  • Charlotte Devereaux Beaumont is deceased (October 2023) and cannot be prosecuted.
  • No other suspects' DNA was found on the scarf, indicating sole perpetrator involvement.
  • The age of the biological material (67 years) resulted in partial DNA degradation, but sufficient loci were recovered for conclusive identification.
