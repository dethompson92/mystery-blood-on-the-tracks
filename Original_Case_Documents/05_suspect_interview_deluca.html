INTERVIEW SUMMARY

Mr. Vincent DeLuca voluntarily submitted to questioning regarding the death of his fiancée, Miss Vivienne Marchand. He was advised that he is not under arrest but is considered a person of interest due to his relationship with the victim and his presence aboard the Silver Comet train. Mr. DeLuca declined legal representation and agreed to answer questions. He appeared visibly distressed and emotional throughout the interview. The following transcript represents the substance of the interview, edited for clarity and brevity.

INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT

DETECTIVE HOLLOWAY: Mr. DeLuca, please state your full name, age, and occupation for the record.
DELUCA: Vincent Anthony DeLuca. I'm forty-seven years old. I own The Blue Moon Jazz Club in the French Quarter of New Orleans. Been running it for twelve years now.
HOLLOWAY: And how did you know Miss Vivienne Marchand?
DELUCA: (voice breaking) She was my fiancée, Detective. We were engaged to be married. The wedding was planned for January 18th, 1958. St. Louis Cathedral in New Orleans. (pause) I can't believe she's gone. This doesn't feel real.
HOLLOWAY: I understand this is difficult, Mr. DeLuca. We appreciate your cooperation. When did you first meet Miss Marchand?
DELUCA: I met Vivienne in Chicago in August of this year—August 1957. I was up there scouting talent for the club. I heard her sing at a jazz lounge on the South Side. She was incredible, Detective. Best voice I'd heard in twenty years in this business. I approached her after her set, introduced myself, offered her a contract to perform at The Blue Moon. She accepted.
HOLLOWAY: And your relationship became romantic?
DELUCA: Yes. We started seeing each other socially while we worked out the contract details. She was beautiful, talented, sophisticated—everything a man could want. I fell for her hard and fast. By the end of September, I proposed. She said yes. We announced the engagement in October.
HOLLOWAY: Why was Miss Marchand traveling to New Orleans on this particular train?
DELUCA: She was moving to New Orleans to begin her residency at my club. She was scheduled to start performing next week—November 20th. We were also planning to finalize wedding details—meeting with the priest, looking at apartments. This trip was supposed to be the beginning of our new life together.
HOLLOWAY: Why were you on the same train? Were you traveling together?
DELUCA: Yes and no. I had business in Chicago—meeting with suppliers for the club. I timed my trip so we could travel back together, but we booked separate compartments. Vivienne wanted her privacy for the journey. She said she needed time to think, to prepare herself mentally for the move. I respected that.
HOLLOWAY: Mr. DeLuca, multiple witnesses have reported that you and Miss Marchand had an argument in the lounge car last night around ten-thirty. Can you tell me about that?
DELUCA: (sighs heavily) Yes. We had a disagreement. I wouldn't call it an argument, exactly, but yes, we had words.
HOLLOWAY: What was the disagreement about?
DELUCA: (long pause) Vivienne had been acting strange for the past few weeks—distant, distracted. Since we announced the engagement, she seemed to be pulling away from me emotionally. I confronted her about it last night. I asked her if she was having second thoughts about the wedding, about moving to New Orleans. She got defensive.
HOLLOWAY: What exactly did you say to her?
DELUCA: I said, "You can't just walk away from this, Vivienne. We have plans. We made commitments." She said, "I can do whatever I choose, Vincent. I'm not your property." It stung, Detective. I felt like she was slipping away from me, and I didn't understand why.
HOLLOWAY: Were you angry?
DELUCA: I was frustrated, confused, hurt. Angry? Maybe a little. But not the kind of angry where I would ever hurt her. I loved her, Detective. I would never lay a hand on her.
HOLLOWAY: How did the conversation end?
DELUCA: She told me she needed space, needed time to think. She said we would talk in the morning. Then she left the lounge car and went back to her compartment. That was the last time I saw her alive. (voice breaking) The last time, Detective.
DETECTIVE'S NOTE: Subject became visibly emotional at this point. Paused interview for five minutes to allow subject to compose himself.
HOLLOWAY: What did you do after Miss Marchand left the lounge car?
DELUCA: I stayed in the lounge. I was upset. I ordered another bourbon and sat there, thinking, trying to figure out what I'd done wrong. I couldn't understand why she was pulling away. I thought maybe she was nervous about the wedding, about leaving Chicago. Cold feet. I thought we'd work it out in the morning.
HOLLOWAY: How long did you remain in the lounge car?
DELUCA: Until about one-thirty in the morning. Maybe closer to one forty-five. I lost track of time.
HOLLOWAY: Can anyone confirm you were there during that time?
DELUCA: The bartender. His name is Marcus, I think. Marcus Webb. He served me drinks. There were a couple other passengers in the lounge too, but I don't know their names. I kept to myself mostly.
DETECTIVE MONROE: Mr. DeLuca, what did you do after you left the lounge car at one-thirty or one forty-five?
DELUCA: I went back to my compartment—Compartment 10 in the Magnolia car—and went to bed. I was exhausted, emotionally drained. I fell asleep pretty quickly.
MONROE: Did you leave your compartment at any time during the night after you went to bed?
DELUCA: No. I slept through the night until the porter knocked on my door around seven in the morning. That's when I found out... (trails off)
HOLLOWAY: Mr. DeLuca, your compartment was Compartment 10, correct? That's only three compartments away from Miss Marchand's compartment, Compartment 7. Did you hear anything unusual during the night? Any sounds, voices, movement in the corridor?
DELUCA: No, nothing. Once I fell asleep, I was out. The train noise, the rocking motion—it all helped me sleep. I didn't hear anything.
HOLLOWAY: Did you know any of the other passengers in the Magnolia sleeper car?
DELUCA: No, I didn't know any of them. Vivienne and I were acquainted with a couple of people—there was a photographer, Maggie something, and a doctor, I think. Vivienne knew them from Chicago. We had dinner with them in the dining car. But I didn't know them personally.
HOLLOWAY: Did you observe Miss Marchand interacting with any other passengers in a way that seemed unusual or noteworthy?
DELUCA: Not really. She was friendly with everyone, but that was just her personality. She was charming, easy to talk to. Everyone liked her.
HOLLOWAY: Mr. DeLuca, I need to ask you directly. Did you kill Vivienne Marchand?
DELUCA: (voice rising) No! Absolutely not! I loved her, Detective. I was going to marry her. Why would I kill her? This is insane. I would never hurt her.
HOLLOWAY: Then help us understand. You had an argument with her. She was pulling away from you. You were frustrated, angry even. You were three compartments away from her during the night. You have to understand how this looks.
DELUCA: I understand how it looks, but I swear to you on my mother's grave, I did not leave my compartment after I went to bed. I did not go to Vivienne's room. I did not touch her. Someone else did this. Someone else killed her.
MONROE: Do you have any idea who might have wanted to harm Miss Marchand? Did she have enemies? Was she involved in anything dangerous?
DELUCA: No, nothing like that. Vivienne was a singer, an artist. She wasn't involved in anything shady. She had no enemies that I knew of. Everyone loved her.
HOLLOWAY: Was she keeping any secrets from you? Anything she didn't tell you about her past or her personal life?
DELUCA: (pause) I don't think so. I mean, we all have things we don't share, but nothing that would lead to murder. She was private about certain things—her past in Paris during the war, her early years in America. She didn't like to talk about it. But secrets? Nothing like that.
HOLLOWAY: Did Miss Marchand ever mention anyone who made her uncomfortable? Anyone who was overly interested in her or pursuing her?
DELUCA: No. She had admirers—she was a beautiful, talented woman—but no one who seemed dangerous or obsessed.
MONROE: Mr. DeLuca, do you have any objection to us searching your compartment and your belongings?
DELUCA: No, search whatever you want. I have nothing to hide.
HOLLOWAY: We'd also like to take your fingerprints for elimination purposes. Is that acceptable?
DELUCA: Yes, fine. Whatever you need to do to find who did this.
HOLLOWAY: One last question, Mr. DeLuca. Miss Marchand was last seen wearing a distinctive burgundy and gold silk scarf. That scarf is now missing. Do you know anything about it?
DELUCA: A scarf? I remember she had a scarf like that—she wore it sometimes when we went out. But I don't know anything about it being missing. I didn't take it, if that's what you're asking.
HOLLOWAY: Where were you between two-thirty and three-thirty this morning?
DELUCA: I already told you—I was asleep in my compartment. Alone. I didn't leave. I didn't go anywhere.
HOLLOWAY: Is there anything else you'd like to add to this statement?
DELUCA: Just that I loved Vivienne. I wanted to spend the rest of my life with her. Whoever did this took everything from me. I hope you find them and make them pay.

INTERVIEW CONCLUSION

Interview concluded at 1147 hours. Subject was cooperative but visibly emotional throughout. Subject's alibi (lounge car until 1:30-1:45 AM, then asleep in compartment) requires verification with bartender Marcus Webb and other lounge car patrons. Subject's compartment and belongings were subsequently searched with his consent. No evidence of blood, struggle, or the missing scarf was found. Fingerprints were taken for comparison with any evidence recovered from victim's compartment.

INVESTIGATIVE ASSESSMENT: Vincent DeLuca remains a primary person of interest due to his relationship with victim, reported argument, proximity to crime scene, and lack of verifiable alibi during estimated time of death. However, subject's emotional response appeared genuine. Motive (jealousy, fear of abandonment) is present. Means (physical strength, access to victim) is present. Opportunity (proximity, overnight timeframe) is present. Further investigation required to confirm or eliminate as suspect.
