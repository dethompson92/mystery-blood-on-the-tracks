RE: Reopening of 1957 Vivienne Marchand Homicide - Significant New Evidence

Executive Summary

I am formally recommending the immediate reopening of the 1957 murder of Vivienne Marchand based on newly discovered evidence that has the potential to solve this 67-year-old cold case.

On November 3, 2024, a private estate executor contacted NOPD regarding the discovery of a locked steamer trunk in a French Quarter residence during estate liquidation. The trunk's contents include items directly related to the Marchand murder, including what appears to be the murder weapon and documentary evidence suggesting the identity of the killer.

Preliminary forensic analysis indicates that DNA evidence can be extracted from the recovered materials. With modern forensic techniques unavailable in 1957, we now have the ability to definitively link these items to the victim and potentially identify the perpetrator.

This case should be reopened immediately. I believe it can be solved within 90 days.

Background - Original 1957 Investigation

Original Incident: On November 14, 1957, at approximately 6:45 AM, Vivienne Marchand, a 32-year-old jazz singer, was found dead in her compartment aboard the Silver Comet passenger train. She had been strangled with a silk scarf while sleeping. The crime occurred somewhere between New Orleans and Jackson, Mississippi.

Original Investigation Summary (Jackson PD, 1957-1958):

  • Lead Investigator: Detective James Holloway (Jackson PD, deceased 1983)
  • Investigation Duration: November 14, 1957 - February 28, 1958 (107 days)
  • Result: UNSOLVED - No arrests, no suspects identified with sufficient evidence
  • Case closed as unsolved, transferred to inactive cold case status February 28, 1958

Key Obstacles in 1957 Investigation:

  1. Locked-Room Mystery: Compartment door was locked from inside when body was discovered—perpetrator's exit method never explained
  2. Missing Murder Weapon: Victim's burgundy-gold silk scarf (identified as probable weapon based on ligature marks) was never recovered despite extensive searches
  3. Unidentified Suspect: Witness saw victim conversing with unidentified woman in burgundy suit at 10:45 PM on night of murder—this woman was never positively identified
  4. Limited Forensic Technology: 1957 forensic capabilities were primitive by modern standards—no DNA analysis, limited fingerprint technology, basic fiber analysis only
  5. Lack of Physical Evidence: Crime scene yielded minimal forensic evidence; perpetrator left virtually no trace

Original Suspect Profile (1957): Jackson PD believed the killer was likely a female passenger who knew the victim personally, possibly connected to victim's past, traveling under false identity. Primary person of interest was the unidentified "woman in burgundy suit" seen with victim hours before murder.

🔍 BREAKTHROUGH: NEW EVIDENCE DISCOVERY - NOVEMBER 3, 2024

Discovery Location: 412 Dauphine Street, French Quarter, New Orleans

Property Context: Historic residence, occupied by Charlotte Devereaux from 1952 until her death on October 15, 2024 (age 95). Estate is being liquidated by executor William Devereaux (nephew).

Discovery Circumstances: Estate executor found locked steamer trunk in attic storage room. Trunk bore no identifying marks. Lock was rusted shut; executor obtained locksmith services to open. Contents appeared to be personal memorabilia from 1950s-1960s era.

Initial Contact with NOPD: Executor recognized one item—a silk scarf with apparent bloodstains—as potentially significant. Upon researching his aunt's background and the scarf's possible connection to a historical case, he contacted NOPD Cold Case Unit on November 3, 2024.

NOPD Response: Det. Lt. Sarah Chen and Det. Marcus Williams responded to scene, secured trunk and contents, transported to NOPD Evidence Storage Facility. Search warrant obtained (Orleans Parish, Judge Martha Fontenot, Case #2024-SW-11034).

Contents of Recovered Trunk - Evidence Inventory

📦 EVIDENCE TAG: EVID-2024-0847-001 (Steamer Trunk & Complete Contents)

ITEM 1: BURGUNDY-GOLD SILK SCARF

Evidence Sub-Tag: EVID-2024-0847-001A

Description: Silk scarf, approximately 36 inches length, burgundy base color with gold metallic thread pattern. High-quality fabric, consistent with 1950s fashion. Shows signs of aging but well-preserved in trunk environment.

CRITICAL OBSERVATION: Scarf exhibits dark staining consistent with aged bloodstains in center section. Staining pattern suggests fabric was wrapped or twisted around object (consistent with use as ligature).

Forensic Status: Submitted for DNA analysis. Preliminary testing confirms presence of biological material (blood). Full DNA extraction and profiling in progress.

Connection to 1957 Case: Matches exact description of victim's missing scarf per witness statements and fiancé's description. Jackson PD identified missing scarf as probable murder weapon in original case file.

ITEM 2: PERSONAL JOURNAL (1957)

Evidence Sub-Tag: EVID-2024-0847-001B

Description: Leather-bound diary dated 1957, handwritten entries spanning January-November 1957. Entries authored by Charlotte "Lottie" Devereaux (deceased property owner). Journal documents personal thoughts, relationships, and activities during 1957.

CRITICAL CONTENT: Multiple entries reference "V.M." (believed to be Vivienne Marchand) in emotional context. Entries from October-November 1957 describe conflict, betrayal, and anguish. Final entry dated November 12, 1957 (two days before murder) contains cryptic statements about "ending the pain" and "she'll never destroy another life."

Evidentiary Value: Establishes direct personal relationship between Charlotte Devereaux and victim Vivienne Marchand. Documents motive and intent. Will be analyzed by forensic linguist and criminal psychologist.

ITEM 3: LOVE LETTERS (1954-1957)

Evidence Sub-Tag: EVID-2024-0847-001C

Description: Collection of 27 handwritten letters, tied with ribbon. Letters dated 1954-1957, addressed to "My Darling Lottie," signed with initial "V." Romantic in nature, documenting secret relationship between two women.

Content Summary: Letters describe hidden romantic relationship, expressions of love, plans for future together, and increasing desperation as societal pressure mounts. Later letters (1956-1957) show deterioration of relationship, references to "V's" engagement to male suitor, pleas from "V" for continued secrecy.

CRITICAL SIGNIFICANCE: Establishes romantic relationship between Charlotte Devereaux and Vivienne Marchand. Provides clear motive: betrayal, abandonment, and fear of exposure in an era when homosexuality was criminalized and socially ruinous.

Handwriting Analysis: Preliminary comparison to known Vivienne Marchand handwriting samples (from original case file) shows strong match. Full forensic document analysis pending.

ITEM 4: TRAIN TICKET STUB

Evidence Sub-Tag: EVID-2024-0847-001D

Description: Illinois Central Railroad ticket stub for Silver Comet Train 47, dated November 13, 1957, New Orleans to Chicago. First-class sleeper accommodation, Magnolia Car, Compartment 2. Ticket issued to "Charlotte L. Devereaux."

CRITICAL SIGNIFICANCE: Places Charlotte Devereaux on the train where murder occurred, in the same sleeper car as victim. This definitively identifies her as the "unidentified woman in burgundy suit" that 1957 investigators could never find. She was using her real name but no one connected her to the victim because their relationship was secret.

ITEM 5: NEWSPAPER CLIPPINGS

Evidence Sub-Tag: EVID-2024-0847-001E

Description: Collection of newspaper articles from November 1957-March 1958, covering Vivienne Marchand's murder and subsequent investigation. Articles from Jackson Clarion-Ledger, Chicago Tribune, New Orleans Times-Picayune. Some articles annotated in margins with handwritten notes.

Behavioral Evidence: Demonstrates suspect's ongoing interest in case, potential guilt, and need to monitor investigation's progress. Common behavior pattern in murderers who evade justice.

ITEM 6: PHOTOGRAPH (circa 1954)

Evidence Sub-Tag: EVID-2024-0847-001F

Description: Black and white photograph showing two women in casual dress, arms around each other, smiling. Inscription on reverse: "Lottie & Viv - Pontchartrain Beach, Summer 1954 - Forever." One woman matches known photographs of Vivienne Marchand.

Evidentiary Value: Visual confirmation of personal relationship between suspect and victim. Contradicts narrative that they were strangers.

Preliminary Analysis & Investigative Theory

Suspect Identification: Charlotte "Lottie" Devereaux (DOB: 3/17/1929, DOD: 10/15/2024)

1957 Background: In November 1957, Charlotte Devereaux was 28 years old, unmarried, from prominent New Orleans family (Devereaux shipping fortune). Lived independently at 412 Dauphine Street. No criminal record. Socially prominent in arts and culture circles. Described by contemporaries as reserved, private, "never married." In era's coded language, this often indicated lesbian orientation.

Connection to Victim: Charlotte and Vivienne were secret romantic partners from approximately 1954-1957. Relationship was hidden due to: (1) criminalization of homosexuality, (2) social stigma and career destruction potential, (3) Vivienne's public career requiring heterosexual appearance, (4) family pressure on both women.

Motive - Betrayal & Abandonment: In 1957, Vivienne Marchand became engaged to Vincent DeLuca (Chicago club owner). This engagement effectively ended her relationship with Charlotte. Letters and journal entries document Charlotte's devastation, sense of betrayal, and fear that Vivienne might expose their relationship. In 1957, exposure would mean: criminal prosecution, social exile, career destruction, family disgrace.

Method & Opportunity:

  • Charlotte boarded same train as Vivienne (both traveling to Chicago)
  • Charlotte in Compartment 2, Vivienne in Compartment 7—same car, close proximity
  • Witness saw Charlotte conversing with Vivienne at 10:45 PM (described as "old friends catching up")
  • Charlotte likely visited Vivienne's compartment late at night, was invited in (victim trusted her)
  • Used Vivienne's own silk scarf (which Charlotte had likely given her as gift) as murder weapon
  • Attack occurred while Vivienne slept or was relaxed/off-guard
  • Charlotte locked door from inside after murder, then exited and returned to her own compartment
  • Retained scarf as keepsake/trophy, concealed it for 67 years until death

Why She Was Never Caught (1957):

  1. Her relationship with victim was completely secret—no one knew to connect them
  2. She used her real name but presented herself as ordinary passenger with no connection to victim
  3. When interviewed, she likely stated she didn't know victim (which investigators had no reason to doubt)
  4. The "woman in burgundy suit" witness description matched Charlotte, but she had likely changed clothes and disposed of burgundy suit, so investigators couldn't match her to description
  5. Her privileged social status and family connections may have insulated her from scrutiny
  6. 1957 investigators would never have imagined a secret lesbian relationship as motive—such relationships were invisible to mainstream society

Post-Crime Life: Charlotte lived remaining 67 years in New Orleans, never married, led private life, involved in arts patronage. No known subsequent violent behavior. Kept trunk of evidence sealed in attic until death. This behavior suggests guilt, memorialization of victim, and fear of discovery.

⚖️ LEGAL & ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS

Suspect is Deceased: Charlotte Devereaux died October 15, 2024. Therefore, criminal prosecution is impossible. However, case closure and historical justice remain important.

Recommended Actions:

  1. Complete DNA Analysis: Extract and profile DNA from scarf. Compare to victim's DNA (if biological samples available from 1957 autopsy materials). Confirm scarf's connection to murder.
  2. Forensic Document Analysis: Verify handwriting in love letters matches victim. Analyze journal entries for psychological profile.
  3. Historical Investigation: Research Charlotte Devereaux's 1957 movements, interview any surviving family members or contemporaries.
  4. Case Closure Report: Prepare comprehensive report documenting solution to 67-year-old cold case.
  5. Family Notification: Contact victim's surviving family members (if any) to provide closure.
  6. Public Disclosure: Work with NOPD Public Affairs to prepare sensitive public statement acknowledging LGBTQ+ historical context.

Historical Context Statement: This case must be presented with sensitivity to historical LGBTQ+ persecution. In 1957, same-sex relationships were criminalized, stigmatized, and socially ruinous. This case represents a tragedy born of societal oppression that forced people into secrecy and desperation. While murder cannot be excused, the context matters for complete understanding.
