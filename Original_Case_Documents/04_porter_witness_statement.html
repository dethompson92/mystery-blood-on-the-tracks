PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

I, Detective James Holloway, Badge Number 402 of the Jackson Police Department Homicide Bureau, hereby attest that on this fourteenth day of November, 1957, at 0845 hours, I conducted a formal interview with Mr. Ernest Washington at the Jackson Union Station Railway Police Office. Mr. Washington was advised that he is being interviewed as a material witness in a homicide investigation and that his cooperation is voluntary. He was not placed under arrest nor advised of rights to counsel as he is not considered a suspect at this time. Mr. Washington stated he understood and agreed to provide a full and truthful account of his observations. The following represents his statement, transcribed from my handwritten notes taken during the interview.

WITNESS STATEMENT (Question and Answer Format)

Q: Mr. Washington, please state your full name, age, and occupation for the record.
A: My name is Ernest Lee Washington. I am forty-three years old. I work as a Pullman porter for the Seaboard Air Line Railroad. I have been employed in this capacity for twenty-two years, sir.
Q: You were the porter assigned to Sleeper Car Number 7, designated "Magnolia," aboard the Silver Comet train that departed Chicago on November 13, 1957, is that correct?
A: Yes, sir. That is correct. Car 7, the Magnolia, is my regular assignment on the Chicago to New Orleans route. I know that car like the back of my hand.
Q: How many passengers were assigned to your car for this particular journey?
A: There were ten compartments, sir. All ten were occupied. I had ten passengers under my care. Well, nine passengers and one couple sharing Compartment 5, so that makes ten total souls.
Q: Did you know any of these passengers prior to this trip?
A: No, sir. They were all strangers to me. Some were regular travelers on the line, but I had not personally served any of them before this journey.
Q: Mr. Washington, I need you to recount in detail your activities and observations from the time the train departed Chicago yesterday afternoon until this morning when you discovered the deceased woman. Take your time and be as specific as possible.
A: Yes, sir. I will do my best. The Silver Comet departed Chicago Union Station at four o'clock in the afternoon on Wednesday, November 13th, right on schedule. My duties as porter include assisting passengers with their luggage, preparing their berths for sleeping, answering calls for service throughout the night, and conducting morning wake-up rounds. I also maintain the cleanliness of the car and ensure passenger comfort.
Q: When did you first become aware of the passenger in Compartment 7, the deceased woman, Miss Vivienne Marchand?
A: I assisted her when she boarded in Chicago. She was traveling alone. She had two pieces of luggage—one large leather suitcase and one smaller vanity case. I carried them to Compartment 7 for her. She was a very elegant lady, well-dressed, spoke with a French accent. She was polite to me, thanked me, and tipped me a dollar right then, which was generous.
Q: Did you observe her interactions with any other passengers during the boarding process or the early part of the journey?
A: Yes, sir. As I was helping her, I noticed a gentleman from Compartment 10—Mr. Vincent DeLuca, I later learned his name—approach her in the corridor. He greeted her warmly, called her by name, "Vivienne." They spoke briefly. He mentioned meeting her in the dining car for supper. She nodded and smiled. They seemed to know each other well, perhaps friends or business acquaintances.
Q: Did you observe anything unusual about Miss Marchand's demeanor or behavior during the afternoon and evening?
A: No, sir. She seemed perfectly normal. She spent most of the afternoon and early evening in the lounge car. I saw her there when I passed through around six o'clock. She was sitting alone, reading a book, smoking a cigarette. Later, around seven-thirty, I saw her in the dining car having supper with Mr. DeLuca and two other passengers—a lady photographer from Compartment 4 and a doctor from Compartment 3. They seemed to be having a pleasant conversation.
Q: What time did Miss Marchand return to her compartment for the night?
A: I believe it was around eleven o'clock, maybe eleven-fifteen. I was making my final evening rounds, checking that all passengers were settled. I saw her walking down the corridor toward Compartment 7. She was wearing her nightclothes—a silk nightgown and a burgundy robe. She also had a scarf around her neck, burgundy and gold colored, very beautiful. She wished me good evening as she passed. I asked if she needed anything before bed, and she said no, she was fine. She went into her compartment and closed the door.
DETECTIVE'S NOTE: Witness specifically recalls seeing the victim wearing the burgundy and gold silk scarf at approximately 11:00-11:15 PM on November 13. This scarf is currently unaccounted for and is believed to be the murder weapon. Witness is the last known person to see victim alive.
Q: After Miss Marchand entered her compartment at eleven-fifteen, did you see or hear anything from her compartment during the night?
A: No, sir, I did not. The compartment was quiet. I did my rounds checking on the other passengers. Around eleven forty-five, I made one final walk through the car to ensure everyone was settled. All the compartment doors were closed, and it was quiet. I retired to my porter's quarters in Compartments 1 and 2 at the front of the car.
Q: Did you sleep through the night, or were you awake at any point?
A: I sleep lightly, sir, as part of my job. Passengers sometimes ring the call bell during the night if they need something. I woke several times during the night. Once around one-fifteen in the morning, I got up to check the car because I thought I heard a door open, but when I looked into the corridor, it was empty and quiet. All the compartment doors were closed.
Q: You mentioned hearing a door. Can you be more specific? Which door?
A: I cannot say for certain, sir. It could have been any of the compartment doors, or perhaps someone going to the washroom at the end of the car. I didn't see anyone, so I assumed it was nothing and went back to my quarters.
Q: Did you wake at any other times during the night?
A: Yes, sir. I woke again around two-fifty in the morning. I remember checking my pocket watch. I heard soft footsteps in the corridor outside my door. The floor creaks a bit when someone walks. I listened for a moment, but I didn't hear anything else—no voices, no disturbance. After a minute or two, it was quiet again. I assumed a passenger was using the washroom. I did not get up to investigate because there was no call bell and no sound of distress.
DETECTIVE'S NOTE: Witness reports hearing footsteps in corridor at approximately 2:50 AM. This timeframe aligns with medical examiner's estimated time of death (2:30-3:30 AM). Possible perpetrator movement.
Q: Did you wake again before morning?
A: Yes, sir, one more time. Around three-ten in the morning, I woke briefly. I noticed a light under the door of Compartment 8—that's the compartment right next to Miss Marchand's, Compartment 7. The light was on, which struck me as a bit unusual for that hour, but passengers sometimes have trouble sleeping on trains. I didn't think much of it. I went back to sleep.
DETECTIVE'S NOTE: Light observed under Compartment 8 door at approximately 3:10 AM. Compartment 8 occupied by Charlotte "Lottie" Devereaux, actress, age 29. Compartment directly adjacent to victim's compartment. Requires follow-up interview.
Q: What time did you begin your morning rounds?
A: I woke at six-thirty in the morning and began my rounds at six forty-five. My routine is to knock softly on each compartment door and offer morning tea or coffee service. I start at Compartment 3 and work my way down to Compartment 10. I don't disturb Compartments 1 and 2 since those are my own quarters.
Q: Walk me through what happened when you reached Compartment 7, Miss Marchand's compartment.
A: I knocked on the door three times—soft knocks, not loud, just enough to wake someone gently. I called out, "Good morning, miss. Porter service. Would you care for morning tea?" There was no answer. I waited about ten seconds and knocked again, a little louder this time. Still no answer. Now, sometimes passengers sleep deeply or don't want to be disturbed, but something felt off to me. The train was due into Jackson in about twenty minutes, and most passengers are awake by then, getting ready to disembark or preparing for the day.
Q: What did you do next?
A: I have a master key that opens all the compartment doors in case of emergency. I used my key to unlock Compartment 7. I opened the door slowly and called out again, "Miss Marchand? Porter service, ma'am." Still no response. I stepped inside.
Q: Describe what you saw when you entered the compartment.
A: (pause) Miss Marchand was lying on the berth bed, on her back. Her hands were folded across her chest, like she was sleeping peacefully. The blanket was pulled up to her waist. Her eyes were closed. For a moment, I thought she was just in a deep sleep. But then I noticed her face. It was... it was pale, sir, too pale. And her lips had a bluish color. I've seen death before. I knew immediately something was terribly wrong.
Q: Did you touch her or move anything in the compartment?
A: I stepped closer and gently touched her hand to check for a pulse or warmth. Her hand was cold, sir. Cold and stiff. I knew she was gone. I did not move anything else. I backed out of the compartment immediately and closed the door. I went straight to the conductor's car and reported what I had found.
Q: Did you notice anything unusual or out of place in the compartment when you entered?
A: The compartment was very tidy, sir. Too tidy, if I may say. It looked like someone had arranged everything neatly. Her suitcase was closed and placed by the wall. Her jewelry box was on the small table, closed. There was a book on the bed near her. Everything seemed orderly. But I remember thinking it was strange because when I checked on her the night before at eleven forty-five, I could hear through the door that she was still moving about, preparing for bed. You'd expect some things to be out of place—clothes, toiletries. But it was all very neat.
Q: You mentioned earlier that Miss Marchand was wearing a burgundy and gold scarf when you last saw her at eleven o'clock. Did you see that scarf in the compartment this morning?
A: (pause) No, sir. I did not see the scarf. I remember it clearly from the night before because it was quite striking—a beautiful piece. But this morning, it was not around her neck, and I did not see it anywhere in the compartment.
DETECTIVE'S NOTE: Missing scarf is critical evidence. Last seen on victim at 11:00 PM. Not present at scene at 6:45 AM. Likely murder weapon removed by perpetrator.
Q: Mr. Washington, did you observe any tensions or conflicts between passengers during the journey? Any arguments or suspicious behavior?
A: Well, sir, there was one incident in the lounge car around ten-thirty last night. Mr. DeLuca—the gentleman from Compartment 10 who seemed to know Miss Marchand—was speaking with her, and their voices were raised. I wouldn't call it a full argument, but it was tense. I was passing through and overheard a bit. He said something like, "You can't just walk away from this," and she replied, "I can do whatever I choose." Other passengers in the lounge noticed. It seemed like a personal matter, so I did not intervene. They lowered their voices after that, and he left the lounge car shortly after.
Q: Did you observe Miss Marchand's demeanor after this exchange?
A: She remained in the lounge for another fifteen or twenty minutes. She seemed composed, but perhaps a bit irritated. She finished her drink and then retired to her compartment, which is when I saw her at eleven o'clock.
Q: Is there anything else you observed during the journey that you believe might be relevant to this investigation?
A: Only that the lady in Compartment 8, Miss Devereaux, seemed quite distressed during the evening. I served her two glasses of bourbon in the lounge car around nine o'clock. She was sitting alone, and she seemed upset—not crying, but withdrawn, staring out the window. When I asked if she needed anything, she just shook her head. I've been a porter for many years, sir, and you learn to read people. She seemed troubled.
Q: Did you observe any interaction between Miss Devereaux and Miss Marchand?
A: Not that I recall, sir. They may have passed each other in the corridor, but I did not see them speaking together.
Q: Mr. Washington, is there anything else you wish to add to this statement?
A: No, sir. I have told you everything I remember. I am deeply sorry that this happened on my car. Miss Marchand seemed like a fine lady. I hope you find whoever did this.

STATEMENT CONCLUSION

This concludes the formal statement of Mr. Ernest Washington, taken on November 14, 1957, at 0845 hours. Mr. Washington was cooperative and forthcoming. He is instructed to remain available for further questioning as the investigation proceeds. He will remain in Jackson, Mississippi, until released by the Jackson Police Department or Seaboard Air Line Railroad management.
