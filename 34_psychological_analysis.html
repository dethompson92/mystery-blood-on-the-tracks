RETROSPECTIVE PSYCHOLOGICAL PROFILE & HOMICIDE ANALYSIS
CASE REFERENCE: NOPD-COLD-2024-0847
DATE OF REPORT: November 28, 2024
SUBJECT (DECEASED): Charlotte Devereaux
VICTIM (DECEASED): Vivienne Marchand
CRIME DATE: November 14, 1957
ANALYSIS TYPE: Post-Mortem Psychological Autopsy

I. INTRODUCTION TO PSYCHOLOGICAL ANALYSIS

Purpose: This report provides a retrospective psychological analysis of the homicide of Vivienne Marchand to understand the behavioral, emotional, and systemic factors that precipitated the crime. By reconstructing the psychological state of both the victim and the perpetrator, we aim to explain the motive and dynamics of this historical cold case.

Methodology: Analysis is based on archival evidence including personal journals (1957), correspondence (1954-1957), witness statements from the original investigation, and the perpetrator's deathbed confession (2024).

Limitations: This analysis is conducted 67 years post-incident. Both subjects are deceased, preventing direct clinical interview. Determinations are based on documented behavior and written records.

II. VICTIM PROFILE: VIVIENNE MARCHAND

PERSONALITY ASSESSMENT High-Functioning, Pragmatic, Compartmentalized

Personality Characteristics

  • Ambitious & Career-Focused: Prioritized professional success and stability above emotional needs.
  • Emotionally Guarded: Demonstrated difficulty with vulnerability; maintained rigid boundaries between her public persona and private life.
  • Pragmatic Survivalist: Made life decisions based on security rather than authenticity (e.g., engagement to Vincent DeLuca).

Psychological Framework

Vivienne displayed an adaptive response to the persecution of the 1950s. Her bisexuality was managed through extreme secrecy and compartmentalization. Her decision to end her relationship with Charlotte Devereaux was driven by "cold pragmatism"—a survival strategy to secure her future through a socially acceptable marriage. She underestimated Charlotte's emotional volatility because she viewed relationships through a rational, transactional lens.

Impact of Historical Trauma: Living in the closet created emotional distance. Fear of exposure shaped all her decisions, leading her to sacrifice authenticity for safety—a common psychological defense mechanism in oppressed groups during this era.

III. KILLER PROFILE: CHARLOTTE "LOTTIE" DEVEREAUX

CLINICAL IMPRESSION Borderline Personality Traits with Anxious-Preoccupied Attachment

Personality Characteristics

  • Emotionally Dependent: Exhibited an insecure attachment style, relying heavily on others for self-worth.
  • Obsessive Fixation: Prone to "limerence" or obsessive love patterns, viewing Vivienne not just as a partner but as a lifeline.
  • Internalized Homophobia: Deep shame regarding her sexuality, manifesting as self-hatred and anxiety.
  • Maladaptive Coping: Documented heavy alcohol use to manage emotional dysregulation.

Mental State Leading to Homicide

Charlotte was in a state of acute emotional crisis. Facing the loss of her primary attachment figure (Vivienne), combined with career failure in Hollywood and financial desperation, she experienced a collapse of her identity. Her journal entries indicate suicidal ideation prior to the murder, suggesting she felt she had "nothing left to lose" except her reputation.

IV. PREMEDITATION VS. CRIME OF PASSION ANALYSIS

Evidence of Premeditation Evidence of Crime of Passion
  • Purchased train ticket after learning of victim's travel.
  • Journal entries show obsessive rumination.
  • Followed victim onto train (stalking behavior).
  • No murder weapon brought to scene (used victim's own scarf).
  • Impulsive decision during heated argument.
  • Immediate shock and remorse (inconsistent with calculated killing).
  • Lack of escape plan (returned to adjacent room).
CLINICAL DETERMINATION CRIME OF PASSION (Voluntary Manslaughter equivalent) with elements of Obsessive Fixation.

The homicide occurred during an acute emotional breakdown triggered by Vivienne's rejection and specific threat to expose Charlotte as "obsessed." This threat represented an existential danger in 1957. The killing was an explosive reaction to betrayal and fear, rather than a cold-blooded execution. Charlotte's 67 years of debilitating guilt are consistent with an unplanned act of violence.

V. TRAUMA AND INTERNALIZED HOMOPHOBIA

Psychological Burden: Charlotte's secret identity created a baseline of constant high anxiety. In 1957, exposure meant social death, imprisonment, and institutionalization. This "existential threat" heightened her fight-or-flight response.

Impact of Persecution: The criminalization of homosexuality meant Charlotte had no legal recourse for a broken heart. She could not seek support, therapy, or validation. When Vivienne threatened exposure, it triggered a survival instinct that overrode inhibition. The murder was, in a twisted psychological sense, an act to "prevent exposure" and survive.

VI. POST-CRIME PSYCHOLOGICAL IMPACT (1957-2024)

Charlotte lived for 67 years with the burden of her crime. Her subsequent life choices reflect a pattern of self-punishment and hiding:

  • Abandonment of Ambition: She never acted again, punishing herself by denying her dream.
  • Performative Normativity: She married a man to construct a "perfect" heteronormative cover, living a lie to prove her innocence.
  • Retention of Evidence: Keeping the scarf and journal suggests an inability to psychologically let go—the items served as a "memorial" to the relationship and a badge of her guilt.
  • Deathbed Confession: The need to confess before death indicates that the guilt remained unresolved and distinct from her self-preservation instinct.

VII. RELATIONSHIP DYNAMICS ANALYSIS

The Fatal Mismatch

The tragedy stemmed from a fundamental incompatibility in attachment needs and power dynamics:

  • Power Imbalance: Vivienne held all the power (success, money, emotional independence), while Charlotte was destitute and dependent.
  • Attachment Clash: Vivienne's Avoidant Attachment (distancing) triggered Charlotte's Anxious Attachment (clinging), creating a cycle of pursuit and withdrawal that escalated to violence.
  • Divergent Realities: Vivienne viewed the affair as a "temporary adventure"; Charlotte viewed it as a "marriage."

VIII. SYSTEMIC FACTORS (Society as Contributing Cause)

It is the clinical opinion of this review that the sociopolitical context of 1957 was a significant contributing factor to this homicide.

If this occurred in 2024: Charlotte would have had access to legal marriage, divorce, therapy, and social support. The breakup would have been painful but likely not fatal.

In 1957 Context: The closet created a high-stakes pressure cooker. The inability to live authentically meant the relationship existed in a vacuum of desperation. The threat of "outing" was weaponized, turning a romantic dispute into a survival scenario.

IX. CLINICAL CONCLUSIONS

RETROSPECTIVE DIAGNOSIS (CHARLOTTE DEVEREAUX) 1. Adjustment Disorder with Disturbance of Conduct (Acute)
2. Borderline Personality Features (Fear of Abandonment)
3. Alcohol Use Disorder (Severe)
4. Unresolved Trauma secondary to Systemic Persecution

Mitigating Factors: Acute emotional distress, intoxication, historical fear of persecution, and provocation (threat of exposure).

Aggravating Factors: Stalking behavior (following victim), concealment of evidence (taking the scarf), and decades of deception.

X. ETHICAL AND THERAPEUTIC REFLECTIONS

Modern Intervention: Had Charlotte received modern mental health intervention, this crime likely would have been prevented. Her warning signs (obsession, depression) were clear but ignored due to the need for secrecy.

Lesson for Practice: This case illustrates how systemic oppression exacerbates individual pathology. When love is criminalized, relationships become dangerous. The "Lavender Scare" did not just destroy careers; in this case, it created the psychological conditions for murder.
