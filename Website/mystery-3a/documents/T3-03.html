I. Audit Mandate

This audit was commissioned by Investigadora Dra. Sofía Montoya-Reyes (FGE-QR) as part of the investigation into the death of Solomon Vance. A USB drive recovered from the possession of production associate Vivian Fong (Exhibit EX-011-USB) contained the complete source code of "EROS" (Emotional Resonance Optimization System), the proprietary algorithm at the center of the Forever Match television production.

Solomon Vance, according to witness statements and digital evidence, had reverse-engineered the algorithm in the weeks prior to his death and threatened to expose it. This audit was conducted to determine the validity of those claims and the system's actual technical nature — which has direct bearing on establishing the motive of multiple suspects, particularly Victor Delgado.

II. What EROS Claims to Be

Marketing materials, production documents, and on-air content describe EROS as follows: "A proprietary machine-learning system that analyzes over 200 biometric and psychometric data points — including heart rate variability, galvanic skin response, pupil dilation during photograph exposure, micro-expression analysis, and 47-dimension personality assessment — to identify neurologically-compatible romantic matches with 94.7% long-term relationship accuracy."

Solara Entertainment Group has invested $4.2 million in production infrastructure attributed to EROS, including biometric scanning hardware, a purported data center in Austin, TX, and a dedicated "AI Lab" set piece. The network has used EROS's supposed scientific credibility as the cornerstone of the show's marketing, commanding premium advertising rates.

III. Source Code Findings

Examination of the EROS v2.3.1 codebase revealed the following:

⚠ Critical Finding — Algorithm Is a Sham

The EROS algorithm is not an AI system. It does not perform machine learning, biometric analysis, or any form of data-driven compatibility calculation. All match scores are pre-assigned by production staff in a manually curated JSON file. The biometric hardware collects data that is immediately discarded. The "scientific explanations" displayed on screen are randomly selected from a pool of pre-written phrases. Solomon Vance's assessment — that the algorithm was fake — was entirely correct.
