Statement of Witness

Detective Holloway: Mr. Henderson, please state your full name and occupation for the record.

Henderson: William Raymond Henderson, but everyone calls me Billy Ray. I'm a bar car attendant for Illinois Central. Been working the Silver Comet route for going on twelve years now.

Detective Holloway: And you were working the bar car on Train 47 departing New Orleans on the night of November 13th?

Henderson: Yes, sir. Started my shift at 8:00 PM when we pulled out of Union Station. Bar car was full that night—always is when we leave New Orleans. Folks like to celebrate their trip with a cocktail or three.

Detective Holloway: Walk me through your movements during the night, particularly between midnight and 4:00 AM.

Henderson: Well, sir, the bar was hopping until about 11:30. Had a good crowd—businessmen, a few ladies traveling together, that photographer fellow who kept ordering gin martinis. Around midnight, things started winding down. By 12:30, I only had maybe four or five stragglers nursing their drinks.

Detective Holloway: Did you see the victim, Miss Vivienne Marchand, in the bar car at any point?

Henderson: The colored singer? No, sir. Never saw her come through. I heard she was on board—word travels fast among the crew—but she didn't stop by the bar. Some of the first-class passengers keep to themselves, especially the entertainers.

Detective Holloway: What time did you close the bar?

Henderson: I called last call at 1:45 AM. By 2:15, I had everyone cleared out. Then I spent about thirty minutes doing my closing routine—washing glasses, wiping down the bar, securing the cash receipts, inventorying the liquor bottles. Railroad's real strict about the inventory. Every drop has to be accounted for.

Detective Holloway: So you finished closing procedures around 2:45 AM?

Henderson: Yes, sir. Maybe closer to 2:50. Then I locked up the bar car and headed back to the crew sleeping quarters in the rear car. That's standard procedure. We're not allowed to leave the bar unattended or unlocked.

Detective Holloway: Did you pass through the sleeper cars to reach the crew quarters?

Henderson: No, sir. The bar car is positioned between the dining car and the coach cars. To get to crew quarters, I walk through the coaches—don't go anywhere near the first-class sleepers. That's a different route entirely. You'd have to go forward through the dining car to reach the Magnolia sleeper car.

Detective Holloway: What time did you reach the crew quarters?

Henderson: I'd say right around 3:00 AM. Maybe a few minutes after. I remember checking my watch because I was thinking about how early we'd be pulling into Jackson.

Detective Holloway: And you remained in the crew quarters for the rest of the night?

Henderson: Yes, sir. I bunked down immediately. Shared the crew car with three other fellas—the assistant conductor, one of the porters from the coach section, and a relief engineer who was deadheading back to Memphis. We were all pretty beat. I didn't hear a thing until the emergency whistle woke us up around 6:45 this morning.

Detective Holloway: Did you notice anything unusual during your shift? Any arguments, suspicious behavior, passengers who seemed agitated or nervous?

Henderson: Nothing out of the ordinary, sir. It was a typical run. Folks were in good spirits—maybe too good spirits after a few drinks, but nothing violent or concerning. Nobody seemed particularly upset or angry. Just the usual banter you get in a bar car.

Detective Holloway: You mentioned a photographer. Can you describe him?

Henderson: Fellow in his fifties, I'd guess. Wore a nice suit, had expensive camera equipment with him in a leather case. Said he was heading to Atlanta for some magazine assignment. Friendly enough. Tipped well. He sat at the bar from about 10:30 until close. We talked a bit about photography—he showed me some of his work. Nothing memorable about him, really.

Detective Holloway: Did he mention anything about the victim or seem interested in her?

Henderson: Not that I recall. We mostly talked about his work—he'd been covering some jazz clubs in New Orleans. Come to think of it, he might have mentioned photographing some performers, but he never specifically said he knew Miss Marchand.

Detective Holloway: One more question, Mr. Henderson. The bar stocks various items—glassware, linens, cocktail napkins. Do you also carry silk items? Bar towels, decorative scarves, anything of that nature?

Henderson: No, sir. Just standard cotton bar towels and cloth napkins. Nothing silk. Railroad provides all our linens—they're plain white or burgundy cotton. Why do you ask?

Detective Holloway: Just covering all the bases. Is there anything else you can think of that might be relevant to this investigation?

Henderson: No, sir. Like I said, it was a quiet night. I'm real sorry about what happened to that lady. Terrible thing. If I'd seen or heard anything suspicious, I'd tell you straight away.

Interviewing Officer's Notes: Witness appears credible and forthcoming. Alibi for the critical timeframe (2:00-4:00 AM) is supported by standard railroad closing procedures and presence in crew quarters with three other crew members. No apparent connection to victim. Witness demonstrated detailed recall of shift activities and timeline. Demeanor was cooperative and appropriately somber. No indicators of deception observed.

Corroborating Information

Bar Car Cash Receipt Log: Closing time documented as 2:15 AM with Henderson's signature. Cash reconciliation completed at 2:47 AM per receipts timestamp.

Crew Sign-In Log: Henderson signed into crew quarters at 3:02 AM on November 14, 1957. Log confirmed by Assistant Conductor Ralph Morrison.

Crew Quarters Witnesses: Three crew members (Morrison, Porter James Tucker, Engineer Harold Phillips) confirm Henderson's presence in crew quarters from approximately 3:00 AM until emergency whistle at 6:45 AM.

Bar Car Inventory: Complete liquor inventory submitted November 14, 1957, showing no discrepancies. Standard closing procedures followed.

Investigative Assessment

Alibi Status: VERIFIED - Multiple crew members and documentary evidence support witness's account of movements and timeline.

Proximity to Crime Scene: Witness's route from bar car to crew quarters does not pass through first-class sleeper section where crime occurred.

Access to Victim: No evidence of contact between witness and victim during journey.

Motive Assessment: No apparent motive. Witness had no known connection to victim.

Follow-Up Required: None. Witness cleared as person of interest.
