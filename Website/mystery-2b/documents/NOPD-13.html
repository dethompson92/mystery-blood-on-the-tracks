Statement of Witness

Detective Holloway: Mr. Blackwood, please state your position with Illinois Central and your duties on Train 47.

Blackwood: I am the head conductor for the Silver Comet, Train 47, operating the New Orleans to Chicago route. I've held this position for sixteen years and have been with Illinois Central for twenty-eight years total. My responsibilities include overseeing all operations aboard the train, supervising crew, ensuring passenger safety, maintaining schedules, and handling any emergencies or unusual situations.

Detective Holloway: How long have you been conductor on this specific route?

Blackwood: The Silver Comet has been my assignment since 1941, with a brief interruption during the war years when I worked freight operations. I returned to this passenger service in 1946. I know this route and this train better than I know my own home.

Detective Holloway: Walk me through your activities on the night of November 13th, from departure until the discovery of Miss Marchand's body.

Blackwood: We departed Union Station in New Orleans at precisely 8:00 PM—on schedule. I conducted my standard departure procedures: ticket verification in all cars, safety briefing for crew, coordination with the engineer regarding our schedule. The train was at approximately 85% capacity, which is typical for a Thursday evening departure.

After departure, I made my rounds through all passenger cars. I check on first-class passengers personally—it's part of the service they pay for. I visited the Magnolia sleeper car around 9:15 PM. I introduced myself to the passengers in that car, as is my custom. Miss Marchand was in Compartment 7. I spoke with her briefly—welcomed her aboard, asked if everything was to her satisfaction. She seemed in good spirits, mentioned she was tired from performances in New Orleans and planned to retire early.

Detective Holloway: Did you notice anything unusual about her demeanor?

Blackwood: Not at all. She was gracious, professional. She did seem fatigued, but that's understandable given her profession. She wasn't nervous or agitated. Nothing gave me any cause for concern.

Detective Holloway: Did you see anyone lingering near her compartment or showing unusual interest in her?

Blackwood: No, sir. The corridor was quiet. A few passengers were still in the dining car at that hour. The Magnolia car has ten compartments, and most passengers were settling in for the night.

Detective Holloway: Continue with your timeline.

Blackwood: I spent the late evening making rounds, checking with the dining car staff during dinner service, conferring with the engineer about our progress. We were running on time. Around 11:00 PM, I did another walk-through of the sleeper cars. Everything was quiet. Lights were dimming in most compartments. No disturbances, no complaints.

At midnight, I retired to my conductor's compartment, which is located in the staff car adjacent to the Magnolia sleeper. I have a small private space there with a desk and a bunk. I typically work on paperwork—manifests, passenger logs, maintenance reports—for an hour or so before sleeping.

Detective Holloway: So you were in your compartment from midnight onward?

Blackwood: Yes, with one exception. Around 2:30 AM, I did a final night check. This is standard procedure on overnight runs—I walk through the passenger cars one last time to ensure everything is secure and quiet. I check door locks, look for any issues that might require attention.

Detective Holloway: This is important, Mr. Blackwood. Did you pass through the Magnolia sleeper car during this 2:30 AM check?

Blackwood: Yes, I did. I walked the full length of the corridor. All compartment doors were closed. I didn't see or hear anything unusual. No sounds of distress, no open doors, no passengers in the corridor. It was completely quiet. I checked the exterior door locks at both ends of the car—both were secure. The entire check took maybe ten minutes.

Detective Holloway: You didn't notice anything concerning in the corridor near Compartment 7?

Blackwood: Nothing. I passed right by it. The door was closed. There was no light showing under the door, which suggested the occupant was asleep. Everything appeared normal.

Detective Holloway: What time did you complete this check?

Blackwood: I returned to my compartment at approximately 2:45 AM. I made a note in my duty log, as I always do. I can show you the entry—it's timed and dated.

Detective Holloway: Did you see any other passengers or crew members during this check?

Blackwood: I saw Porter Washington in the coach section—he was attending to a passenger who needed extra blankets. That would have been around 2:35 AM. Otherwise, no. Everyone was bedded down for the night, as expected.

Detective Holloway: When did you next become aware of a problem?

Blackwood: At 6:42 AM, Porter Washington knocked on my compartment door in an extremely agitated state. He informed me that he had discovered a deceased female passenger in Compartment 7 of the Magnolia car. I immediately accompanied him to the scene. Upon seeing the condition of Miss Marchand, I recognized this was a police matter. I secured the compartment, instructed Washington to prevent anyone from entering that car, and went directly to the engineer to halt the train. We were approximately fifteen minutes outside of Jackson at that point.

Detective Holloway: What did you observe in the compartment?

Blackwood: Miss Marchand was lying on the bed in her nightclothes. She was clearly deceased—no signs of life. The compartment appeared orderly, no signs of struggle or violence. The door had been locked from the inside. Porter had used his master key to enter after receiving no response to his wake-up knock.

Detective Holloway: You saw no evidence of forced entry or disturbance?

Blackwood: None whatsoever. That's what was so deeply unsettling. It appeared as though she had simply gone to sleep and... didn't wake up. But of course, when I saw her face more closely, I could see something was terribly wrong.

Detective Holloway: Mr. Blackwood, in your twenty-eight years with the railroad, have you ever had a homicide occur on one of your trains?

Blackwood: Never. We've had medical emergencies, natural deaths—heart attacks, that sort of thing—but never a murder. This is unprecedented in my career. I've transported tens of thousands of passengers safely. This is... this is a terrible tragedy.

Detective Holloway: Do you know of any conflicts among the passengers in the Magnolia car? Any arguments, tension, suspicious behavior?

Blackwood: None that were reported to me. First-class passengers tend to be courteous and private. I heard no complaints, no reports of disturbances from that car during the entire journey.

Detective Holloway: The victim's fiancé, Vincent DeLuca, was ticketed in a different car. Were you aware of this?

Blackwood: I was not aware they were traveling together until after the incident. Passengers don't always inform crew of their relationships. It's not unusual for traveling companions to book separate accommodations if first-class berths are limited.

Detective Holloway: Is there anything else you can tell me that might be relevant?

Blackwood: Only that I deeply regret that this happened on my train, under my watch. If there was any way I could have prevented this tragedy, I would have. I've reviewed my actions repeatedly in my mind, and I cannot identify any warning signs I missed or any mistakes in procedure. Everyone in that sleeper car will have been within close proximity to one another for the entire night. Whoever did this... they were in that car.

Interviewing Officer's Notes: Witness extremely credible. 28-year railroad veteran with exemplary service record. Demonstrated detailed knowledge of train operations and timeline. Appears genuinely distressed by the incident. His 2:30 AM security check places him in the Magnolia sleeper car during the estimated time window of the murder (2:00-4:00 AM), but he observed nothing suspicious. This suggests the crime occurred very quietly or that the victim was already deceased when he passed by. Witness's account is consistent with physical evidence and other witness statements.

Investigative Assessment

Witness Credibility: VERY HIGH - Veteran railroad employee with no disciplinary record and no apparent motive.

Critical Observation: Witness passed directly through crime scene corridor at approximately 2:40 AM and observed nothing unusual. This narrow timeframe is significant to the investigation.

Access to Crime Scene: Witness possesses master key to all compartments (standard for head conductor) but has no motive and solid professional reputation.

Knowledge of Train Operations: Witness confirmed all exterior doors to Magnolia car were locked during night check, supporting theory that perpetrator was already inside the sleeper car.

Follow-Up Required: Review conductor's duty log entries; verify timeline with Porter Washington's account.
