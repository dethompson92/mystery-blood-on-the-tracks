67-Year-Old Murder Mystery Solved: Woman Confesses to 1957 Train Killing

Breakthrough in "Silver Comet" murder reveals a hidden history of forbidden love and fear during the Lavender Scare era.
By MARCUS WILLIAMS, Crime Reporter | Updated: Dec 2, 2024 at 9:45 AM CST

NEW ORLEANS — For nearly seven decades, the murder of jazz singer Vivienne Marchand aboard the Silver Comet passenger train remained one of the South's most perplexing cold cases. Today, New Orleans police announced the mystery is finally solved.

Charlotte "Lottie" Devereaux, a 95-year-old former actress and New Orleans socialite, confessed to the November 1957 strangulation just six days before her death from cancer last week.

The confession was corroborated by a trove of physical evidence discovered in a locked steamer trunk in Devereaux's attic, including the long-missing murder weapon—a burgundy and gold silk scarf belonging to the victim.

"This case was never just about a murder. It was about a love that society deemed criminal."

"Modern forensic analysis confirmed the presence of the victim's DNA on the scarf found in Ms. Devereaux's possession," stated NOPD Detective Lt. Sarah Chen at a press conference this morning. "This physical evidence, combined with Ms. Devereaux's detailed written confession and personal journals, leaves no doubt as to her culpability."

A Motive Buried in History

The motive for the crime, investigators revealed, was rooted in the intense homophobia of the 1950s. Devereaux and Marchand had been secret romantic partners for three years. When Marchand ended the relationship to marry a man for financial security, Devereaux—fearing exposure and heartbroken—followed her onto the train.

"It was a crime of passion committed in an era of persecution," said District Attorney Jason Williams. "Ms. Devereaux lived in fear that her sexual orientation would destroy her life. That fear, combined with jealousy, led to a tragedy that silenced a talented artist."

Justice Delayed

While Devereaux cannot be prosecuted due to her death, authorities have officially closed the case as solved. Distant relatives of Vivienne Marchand in France issued a statement expressing relief.

"We grew up hearing stories of our great-aunt's mysterious death," the statement read. "To finally know the truth brings peace. We choose to remember Vivienne for her music, not just her tragic end."

The LGBTQ+ community has reacted with mixed emotions, noting how the "Lavender Scare"—the federal government's persecution of gay employees in the 1950s—created a climate where honesty was impossible.

"This tragedy is a stark reminder of the cost of the closet," said a representative from the Louisiana Transgender & Queer History Collective. "Vivienne and Charlotte were both victims of a society that refused to let them exist authentically."

HISTORICAL CONTEXT
The Lavender Scare: Understanding 1957

To understand why Charlotte Devereaux hid her relationship with the victim for 67 years, one must understand the "Lavender Scare."

  • Federal Ban: In 1953, President Eisenhower signed an order banning gay people from federal jobs.
  • Criminal Status: Sodomy was a felony in every state in 1957. Exposure meant prison.
  • Social Ruin: Being "outed" meant losing family, career, and housing.

Progress: Today, same-sex marriage is legal, and employment discrimination is banned.

FORENSIC SCIENCE
How DNA Cracked the Code

In 1957, police couldn't link the missing scarf to the killer even if they found it. Today:

  • Touch DNA: Skin cells left on fabric can identify who handled an object.
  • Preservation: The trunk's dry, dark environment preserved the DNA for decades.
  • Comparison: This technique has solved dozens of cold cases, including the Golden State Killer.
