JAZZ SINGER FOUND DEAD ABOARD LUXURY TRAIN

Police Suspect Foul Play in Death of Vocalist Vivienne Marchand
By CLARENCE MITCHELL, Staff Writer
JACKSON, Miss. — Tragedy struck the rails of the Seaboard Air Line Railroad early yesterday morning when the body of acclaimed jazz vocalist Vivienne Marchand, 32, was discovered in her private compartment aboard the Silver Comet passenger train.

Authorities in Jackson have detained the train and its passengers while an investigation is underway. Police Chief Roy Thornton confirmed that the death is being treated as a "probable homicide," though he declined to elaborate on the cause of death pending a coroner’s report.

Miss Marchand, a French-born chanteuse known for her sultry voice and glamorous stage presence, was traveling from Chicago to New Orleans for a highly anticipated engagement at The Blue Moon club in the French Quarter. She was discovered at approximately 6:45 a.m. by a Pullman porter attempting to deliver a wake-up call.
"We are shocked and devastated by this tragedy on our line."

"The door was locked from the inside," stated a railroad official who wished to remain anonymous. "The porter had to use his master key. It was a terrible shock."

Passengers in the sleeper car "Magnolia" reported hearing nothing unusual during the night as the train traversed Tennessee and Mississippi. Among those detained for questioning is Mr. Vincent DeLuca, 47, a New Orleans nightclub owner and Miss Marchand’s fiancé.

Miss Marchand fled war-torn Paris in 1943 and built a successful career in the jazz clubs of Chicago and New York. Her sudden death has sent shockwaves through the music community.

Monday, November 18, 1957 Jackson Daily News Page 3

POLICE BAFFLED IN TRAIN MURDER CASE

"Locked Room Mystery" Stumps Investigators; Weapon Missing
[PHOTO OF TRAIN]
The Silver Comet sitting at the station where the investigation continues.
INVESTIGATORS admitted yesterday that they face a baffling puzzle in the death of Vivienne Marchand. Coroner Dr. A.J. Taggart confirmed that the singer died of strangulation, likely between 2:00 a.m. and 4:00 a.m. Thursday.

"The peculiar aspect," stated Detective James Holloway of the Jackson Police Department, "is that the compartment door was bolted from the inside. There is no other point of entry."

Police have interviewed over 30 passengers and crew members. A thorough search of the train failed to produce the murder weapon, believed to be a burgundy and gold silk scarf belonging to the victim.

"We are pursuing several leads," Holloway insisted, brushing aside rumors that the case had hit a dead end. Mr. Vincent DeLuca, the victim's fiancé, was questioned extensively and released late Saturday. He maintains he was in the lounge car during the critical hours.

Miss Marchand’s luggage showed no signs of robbery. Her jewelry and cash were untouched, leading police to rule out theft as a motive. This leaves a chilling question: Who wanted the glamorous songstress dead?

POLICE SEEK "MYSTERY WOMAN" IN TRAIN MURDER

$500 Reward for Information Leading to Identification
NEW ORLEANS — Police have issued an urgent appeal for a woman seen speaking with murder victim Vivienne Marchand hours before her death.

A witness aboard the Silver Comet has described a woman in a "distinctive burgundy suit" conversing with Miss Marchand near her compartment around 10:45 p.m. on the night of the murder. This woman has not come forward, and her name does not appear on the official passenger manifest under that description.

"This woman may have critical information regarding the victim's state of mind or final movements," said Detective Holloway. "We urge her to contact authorities immediately."

Friday, December 20, 1957 Jackson Daily News Page 5

TRAIN MURDER INVESTIGATION CONTINUES

ONE MONTH after the shocking murder of Vivienne Marchand, police admit that new leads are scarce.

"We have interviewed every passenger from Car 7 multiple times," said Chief Thornton. "We have checked alibis in three states. It is a frustrating case."

The lack of physical evidence continues to hamper efforts. The missing scarf—the alleged murder weapon—has never been found. Without it, linking a suspect to the crime is proving difficult.

Miss Marchand was laid to rest in Chicago on November 20th. Friends described her as a "talented, beloved performer" who had no known enemies. "She lived for her music," said a fellow musician. "It makes no sense."
Wednesday, January 15, 1958 Jackson Daily News Page 2

MARCHAND CASE GROWS COLD

TWO MONTHS have passed, and the trail of the "Silver Comet Killer" has grown ice cold.

Investigators express private frustration with the case. "Without the weapon or a positive identification of the mystery woman, our hands are tied," a source close to the investigation revealed.

Witnesses have scattered across the country, making follow-up interviews nearly impossible. The "Locked Room" nature of the crime has led some to dub it a "perfect crime," a label police vehemently reject.

"We are not closing the file," Det. Holloway insisted. "But active daily investigation is being scaled back."
Friday, February 28, 1958 Jackson Daily News Final Edition

TRAIN MURDER CASE OFFICIALLY CLOSED UNSOLVED

AFTER 107 DAYS of exhaustive investigation, the Jackson Police Department has officially suspended active inquiry into the murder of Vivienne Marchand.

"We have exhausted every avenue," stated Detective James Holloway in a press conference yesterday. "We have chased every lead, interviewed every witness, and analyzed every scrap of evidence. At this time, we have insufficient evidence to charge any individual."

The investigation clocked over 200 man-hours and spanned four states. Yet, the killer of the 32-year-old jazz singer remains free.
"Perhaps someday, new evidence will emerge to shine a light on the truth."

The Seaboard Air Line Railroad has resumed normal operations. Mr. Vincent DeLuca, the victim's fiancé, has announced the establishment of a memorial fund for young musicians in Miss Marchand's name.

"I will never stop hoping for justice," DeLuca told reporters. "Vivienne deserves that."

For now, however, the mystery of what happened in Compartment 7 remains a secret known only to the killer—and the silent rails of the Silver Comet.

OBITUARY

Chicago Tribune, November 17, 1957
[PHOTO]
MARCHAND, Vivienne (1925-1957)

Vivienne Marie Marchand, 32, passed away suddenly on November 14, 1957. Born in Paris, France, she emigrated to the United States in 1943.

A gifted vocalist, she graced the stages of Chicago's finest jazz clubs and was renowned for her interpretations of French chansons. She was traveling to New Orleans to begin a new chapter of her life and career.

She is survived by her parents, Pierre and Solange Marchand of Lyon, France, and her fiancé, Mr. Vincent DeLuca of New Orleans.

A memorial service will be held at St. James Cathedral this Tuesday. Her voice was silenced too soon, but her melody lingers on.
